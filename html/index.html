What are biologics?
Why biologics are effective.
Biologics are the leading edge.

  • Biologics are treatments forcritically ill patients.

    Patients turn to biologics when traditional treatments fail for conditions like cancer, anemia due to chemotherapy, rheumatoid arthritis, Crohn's disease, psoriasis, HIV/AIDS and multiple sclerosis. Biologics provide proteins that healthy individuals have but patients lack, or they target genetic mutations.

  • Biologics are as unique as the patients they're made for.

    Unlike traditional pills, biologics aren't made from simple, replicatable chemical processes. Biologics are complex treatments made from living cells, which means no two are identical. The final result is a customized treatment for each patient.

  • Biologics have the potential to change more lives.

    Biologics are already FDA-approved for more than 200 serious and difficult-to-treat diseases, and hundreds of other biologic treatments are in development.

Biologics aren't like traditional medicine.

Biologics differ from traditional medicine in three key ways: size, complexity and creation process. Large molecule drugs are often used to treat serious illness—like human growth hormone for HIV/AIDS patients—but biologics are in the range of eight times this size. The size of a biologic is related to its complexity as a living, growing entity, making it impossible to precisely manufacture the same way chemical pills are produced.

Biosimilars are reverse engineered.

Biosimilars attempt to replicate biologics—but they're created from different cell lines by different companies using different processes. Reverse engineering the biologics creation process is imperfect because of the sheer complexity inherent to biologic medicine.

Biosimilars are not generic equivalents.

Chemically produced pills can be safely and effectively genericized the way ice cubes can be replicated consistently in a tray. Biologics, however, are like snowflakes. Each is unique and highly sensitive to alterations of its environment.

Substitution of biosimilars can put patients at risk.

Biologics are nearly impossible to replicate precisely, and scientists are still working to determine which differences in biosimilars cause adverse reactions. Manufacturing differences can lead to differences in patients' reactions, sometimes with serious side effects.

Interchangability is under review.

The FDA is currently working on rules for the interchanagability of biosimilars for biologics. This includes determining whether a biosimilar is expected to be as safe and effective as a biologic.

Ensuring patient safety must come first.

Substitution of a biosimilar for a biologic should only occur if the FDA determines interchangability is sound, and prescribing physicians should be able to prevent substitutions.

Substitutions should be monitored.

Transparency about substitution is critical for monitoring effects on patients. Both physiciansand patients should be notified about a substitution, and pharmacists should keep records of substitutions in order to accurately track adverse reactions.

Like biologics themselves, this issue is complex.
